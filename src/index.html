MODULE 01
Semiconductor Basics
Understanding the foundation of all digital electronics — from silicon atoms to PN junctions.
Foundation Physics No Prerequisites
Why This Matters

Before we can design chips, we need to understand why silicon behaves the way it does. Every transistor, every logic gate, every flip-flop you place in a design is ultimately a precise arrangement of doped silicon. Without this foundation, physical design is just button-clicking — with it, you can make intelligent decisions about timing, power, and yield.

ENGINEER'S NOTEIn interviews at TSMC, Intel, Qualcomm — basic semiconductor physics is always tested even for PD engineers. Know it cold.

Interview Questions — Semiconductor Basics
What is a semiconductor? Why is silicon preferred?+
A semiconductor has electrical conductivity between a conductor and insulator. Silicon is preferred because: (1) Its 1.12 eV bandgap allows room-temperature operation. (2) Its native oxide (SiO₂) is an excellent insulator for gate dielectric. (3) It's abundantly available. (4) Silicon wafer technology is extremely mature and cost-effective.
Explain N-type and P-type doping. What dopants are used?+
N-type: Add Group V atoms (Phosphorus, Arsenic) which donate extra electrons. Majority carriers = electrons. P-type: Add Group III atoms (Boron) which create holes. Majority carriers = holes. The dopant concentration (10¹⁵–10²⁰ /cm³) controls resistivity and transistor threshold voltage.
What is depletion region? How does it affect MOSFET operation?+
The depletion region at a PN junction is a zone depleted of free carriers due to diffusion and built-in electric field. In a MOSFET, the gate voltage modulates a depletion region under the gate oxide, eventually forming an inversion layer (channel) that allows current flow between source and drain. This is the fundamental transistor switching mechanism.
Why are electron mobility and hole mobility different? How does this affect CMOS sizing?+
Electrons experience less lattice scattering than holes due to their lighter effective mass, giving ~3× higher mobility. This makes NMOS transistors faster than PMOS for the same size. In CMOS design, PMOS transistors are sized 2–3× wider than NMOS to achieve matching drive currents and equal rise/fall times.

Module 1 Summary

  • Silicon is a Group IV element with 4 valence electrons, forming a crystal lattice
  • Intrinsic silicon has equal electrons and holes; too resistive for direct use
  • N-type doping (Group V) adds electrons; P-type doping (Group III) adds holes
  • PN junction creates a depletion region with built-in potential ~0.6V
  • Forward bias → conducts; Reverse bias → blocks (diode behavior)
  • RC product determines delay; engineers minimize R and C throughout design

Interview Questions — CMOS
Why is PMOS connected to VDD and NMOS to GND?+
PMOS is a good switch for passing logic HIGH (VDD) because it passes VDD without threshold drop — VGS can reach 0V (gate = source = VDD) when fully OFF, and pulls output all the way to VDD when ON. NMOS is better for pulling to GND. If roles were swapped, you'd get degraded logic levels (PMOS passing GND would degrade to Vtp above GND).
What are the regions of MOSFET operation?+
Cut-off: VGS < Vth, transistor OFF, only leakage current flows.
Linear/Triode: VGS > Vth and VDS < VGS − Vth. Transistor acts like a resistor. Used in pass gates.
Saturation: VGS > Vth and VDS ≥ VGS − Vth. Current is ~constant (pinch-off). This is the amplification/switching region for logic gates.
Why is CMOS preferred over NMOS-only or PMOS-only logic?+
CMOS eliminates the resistive pull-up of NMOS logic, so there's no DC path from VDD to GND in steady state — dramatically lower power. CMOS also provides full rail-to-rail output swing (0 to VDD), better noise margins, and scales well to smaller nodes. NMOS-only is faster but power-hungry; CMOS combines speed with low power.

Module 2 Summary

  • NMOS: N-channel, turns ON for VGS > Vth (+ve), connected to GND, faster
  • PMOS: P-channel, turns ON for VGS < Vtp (−ve), connected to VDD, slower
  • CMOS inverter: PMOS pulls up, NMOS pulls down — never both ON simultaneously
  • Propagation delay = f(transistor drive strength, load capacitance)
  • Dynamic power = α·C·V²·f — reduce V first for maximum power savings
  • Static leakage is dominant in modern sub-28nm nodes

3.1 Combinational vs Sequential Logic
Combinational Logic
  • Output depends ONLY on current inputs
  • No memory element (no state)
  • Examples: AND, OR, NOT, MUX, decoder, adder, ALU
  • Propagation delay: inputs → outputs
  • Analyzed with truth tables & Boolean algebra
Sequential Logic
  • Output depends on current inputs + PAST state
  • Has memory (flip-flops, latches)
  • Examples: Counters, registers, FSMs, FIFOs
  • Clocked — state updates on clock edge
  • Analyzed with state machines & timing diagrams

Interview Questions — Digital Design
What is setup time and hold time? What causes violations?+
Setup time (tsu): Minimum time data must be stable BEFORE the active clock edge for reliable capture. Violation occurs when the combinational path is too slow (data arrives too late).
Hold time (th): Minimum time data must remain stable AFTER the active clock edge. Violation occurs when path is too fast (data changes before FF has captured it). Hold violations are fixed by adding delay buffers — they cannot be fixed by changing frequency.
Difference between synchronous and asynchronous reset?+
Synchronous reset: reset is sampled only at clock edge; simpler timing analysis, needs clock to be running. Asynchronous reset: reset takes effect immediately regardless of clock; works during power-up but can cause metastability at deassertion if not synchronized. Best practice: assert async (immediate), deassert synchronously (synchronizer circuit at deassertion).
What is metastability? How do synchronizers prevent it?+
Metastability occurs when a flip-flop's input violates setup or hold time — it enters an undefined voltage state between 0 and 1. The FF may resolve to 0 or 1 after an unpredictable time. Synchronizers use two or more flip-flops in series: the first may go metastable but resolves during the time between stage 1 and stage 2 (one full clock period of settling time), making the probability of metastability propagation extremely low.

Module 3 Summary

  • Combinational logic: output = f(inputs only). Sequential: output = f(inputs + state)
  • D flip-flop captures D at clock edge; tsu and th must be met for correct operation
  • Setup violations: path too slow → fix by optimization or frequency reduction
  • Hold violations: path too fast → fix by adding delay buffers (critical in PD!)
  • Synchronous reset = sampled at clock edge; async reset = immediate effect
  • Metastability: undefined FF state from timing violation; synchronizers mitigate CDC issues

4.3 Blocking vs Non-Blocking Assignments
Blocking ( = )
always @(*) begin a = b; // executes first c = a; // sees new value of a end
  • Executes in sequence, one at a time
  • Use for combinational logic
  • Simulates like software
Non-Blocking ( <= )
always @(posedge clk) begin a <= b; // scheduled c <= a; // uses OLD value of a end
  • All RHS evaluated first, then assigned
  • Use for sequential (clocked) logic
  • Models real flip-flop behavior
GOLDEN RULEALWAYS use <= (non-blocking) in clocked always blocks. ALWAYS use = (blocking) in combinational always blocks. Mixing them causes simulation-synthesis mismatch — the #1 RTL bug.
4.4 Finite State Machine (FSM)

FSMs are the control backbone of digital design. Two types: Mealy (output depends on state + input) and Moore (output depends only on state).

VERILOG — Moore FSM// 3-state traffic light FSM module traffic_fsm ( input clk, rst, output reg [1:0] light // 00=RED 01=GREEN 10=YELLOW ); localparam RED=2'd0, GREEN=2'd1, YELLOW=2'd2; reg [1:0] state, next; // State register always @(posedge clk) begin if (rst) state <= RED; else state <= next; end // Next state logic (combinational) always @(*) begin case (state) RED : next = GREEN; GREEN : next = YELLOW; YELLOW: next = RED; default: next = RED; endcase end // Output logic (Moore) assign light = state; endmodule
BEST PRACTICEAlways include a default case in case statements. Without it, synthesis may create latches (unintentional memory). Add // synthesis full_case parallel_case only when you understand the implications.

Interview Questions — RTL Design
What is the difference between blocking and non-blocking assignments?+
Blocking (=) executes sequentially within an always block — each statement waits for the previous to complete. Used for combinational logic. Non-blocking (<=) evaluates all RHS first, then assigns all LHS simultaneously after the time step — models hardware flip-flop behavior. Used for sequential (clocked) logic. Mixing them incorrectly causes simulation vs synthesis mismatch, the #1 RTL bug type.
What causes an unintentional latch in RTL? How to avoid?+
An unintentional latch is inferred when a combinational always block doesn't assign an output in all possible conditions (missing else, missing case branch). The synthesizer creates a latch to hold the previous value. To avoid: (1) Always use a default assignment at the start of the always block. (2) Ensure all if/case branches assign all outputs. (3) Use a default case. Latches in synchronous design cause timing analysis failures and unpredictable behavior.
Mealy vs Moore FSM — which is better?+
Moore FSM: output depends only on current state — simpler, glitch-free output. Mealy FSM: output depends on state AND input — faster response (1 cycle earlier), fewer states needed, but outputs can glitch. In practice: Moore for outputs driving clocked logic (clean), Mealy for handshake protocols needing minimum latency. Both are equally valid; the choice depends on the application.

Module 4 Summary

  • RTL = Register Transfer Level — data flowing between registers through combinational logic
  • Non-blocking (<=) for sequential; Blocking (=) for combinational — never mix incorrectly
  • FSM = state register + next-state logic + output logic (three-always-block style is cleanest)
  • Always include default in case statements to prevent latches
  • Avoid #delays, initial blocks, $display in synthesis code
  • Datapath = computation; Control = sequencing. Both together = complete RTL design

5.1 Why Verification Matters

A bug found in RTL takes minutes to fix. The same bug found after tapeout costs $1–50 million to re-spin the chip. Verification is 60–70% of the total design effort in modern SoC projects. More engineers work on verification than on RTL design.

Interview Questions — Verification
What is the difference between simulation and formal verification?+
Simulation applies specific input stimuli and checks outputs — it can only find bugs that the test actually exercises. Formal verification mathematically proves properties hold for ALL possible inputs using model checking or theorem proving — exhaustive but computationally expensive. Simulation is scalable to large designs; formal is most powerful for control-heavy blocks, protocols, and security properties.
What is code coverage vs functional coverage?+
Code coverage measures how much of the RTL code was executed during simulation (lines, branches, toggles). High code coverage means your tests ran through the code. Functional coverage measures whether specific design scenarios you care about were exercised (e.g., "was the FIFO ever full?", "was the state machine in all states?"). 100% code coverage does not guarantee functional correctness — you also need functional coverage to verify intent, not just implementation.

Module 5 Summary

  • Functional verification finds bugs before tapeout — fixing in RTL costs minutes, in silicon costs millions
  • Testbench = DUT instantiation + stimulus + self-checking checker
  • Simulator tools: Synopsys VCS, Cadence Xcelium, Mentor ModelSim/Questa
  • SVA assertions automatically check properties every clock cycle
  • Code coverage = how much code ran; Functional coverage = which scenarios occurred
  • Verification is 60–70% of design effort in modern SoC projects

Interview Questions — Synthesis
What is the difference between compile and compile_ultra in Design Compiler?+
compile is the basic synthesis command — single-pass optimization. compile_ultra is an advanced command that runs multiple optimization passes, uses architectural-level and gate-level optimization, retiming, and hierarchical flow. compile_ultra achieves better QoR (Quality of Results) but takes longer runtime. In production, compile_ultra is almost always used.
What is a false path? Give an example.+
A false path is a timing path that exists in the netlist but is not a real functional path — either it's never activated simultaneously, or the timing relationship doesn't matter. Examples: (1) Reset path — async reset doesn't need timing from/to clock. (2) Scan chain paths during normal operation. (3) Paths between different modes that are never simultaneously active. set_false_path removes these from timing analysis, preventing the tool from optimizing them unnecessarily.
What is a multi-cycle path (MCP)?+
A multi-cycle path is a timing path where the data takes more than one clock cycle to propagate by design. For example, a divider that takes 4 cycles to compute is constrained with set_multicycle_path 4 -setup. This relaxes the timing requirement, allowing the tool to use smaller, more efficient cells instead of brute-force larger drives. MCPs are powerful for area/power savings but must be carefully validated in simulation.

Interview Questions — Library Files
What is the difference between .lib and .lef?+
.lib (Liberty) is a timing model — it describes cell logic function, timing arcs (setup/hold/propagation), power consumption, and capacitance at specific PVT corners. Used by synthesis and STA tools. .lef (Library Exchange Format) is a physical model — it describes cell dimensions, pin locations, routing blockages. Used by place-and-route tools. You need both: .lib to optimize timing, .lef to physically place and route the cells.
What is SPEF? Why is post-layout STA different from pre-layout?+
SPEF (Standard Parasitic Exchange Format) captures the actual RC parasitics of routed wires extracted from the physical layout. Pre-layout STA uses wire load models (statistical estimates based on fanout and technology). Post-layout STA uses real SPEF data — much more accurate. The difference can be 20–40% in net delays, which is why pre-layout timing closure doesn't guarantee post-layout closure. SPEF-based STA is required for signoff.

Interview Questions — Floorplanning
What is utilization? What happens if it is too high or too low?+
Utilization = (Standard cell area + macro area) / Core area × 100%. Too high (>80%): routing becomes congested, DRC violations multiply, timing degrades because detour routes add capacitance. Too low (<50%): die area is wasted, chip cost increases, yield is fine but profit is hurt. Target 65–75%. For congestion-prone designs (complex controllers), go lower. For area-critical designs (mobile SoCs), push higher with careful planning.
What is IR drop and how does it affect timing?+
IR drop is the voltage drop across power grid resistance when current flows. V_local = VDD − (I × R_grid). When local VDD drops, transistors switch slower (drain current ∝ (VGS−Vth)²), increasing cell delay. A 50mV drop can slow cells by 5–10%, converting a timing-met design to a failing one in silicon. Solutions: wider power stripes, more vias, decoupling caps, and reduced current peaks through activity balancing.

9.2 Placement Concepts
Global Placement

Cells are placed approximately to minimize total wire length (HPWL). Overlaps are allowed at this stage. The optimizer uses algorithms like simulated annealing or force-directed placement.

Legalization

All cells are snapped to legal positions: aligned to placement rows, no overlaps, inside core area. Cells must sit on valid row sites with correct orientation (N/FN for adjacent rows).

Congestion

Routing congestion = more wire demand than routing resources in a region. Congestion during placement predicts routing DRC failures. Fix by: spreading cells, adjusting macro placement, or reducing utilization locally.

Timing-Driven Placement

Cells on critical paths are placed closer together to reduce wire delay. The placer weights critical nets more heavily during optimization. Pre-CTS timing uses virtual clocks with estimated uncertainty.

Interview Questions — Placement
What is legalization in placement?+

(5) Use layer promotion — allow routing on more metal layers in the congested area. Congestion that isn't fixed at placement will cause DRC violations after routing.

Interview Questions — CTS
What is clock skew? How does it affect setup and hold timing?+
Clock skew = difference in clock arrival times between launch and capture flip-flops. Positive skew (capture clock later than launch): helps setup timing (gives extra time) but hurts hold. Negative skew (capture earlier): hurts setup, helps hold. Setup slack = T_clock − T_data − T_skew. Hold slack = T_data + T_skew − T_hold_min. CTS targets near-zero skew to maximize both margins simultaneously.
Why are inverter pairs (not buffers alone) used in clock trees?+
Inverter pairs (two inverters = non-inverting) are preferred over single buffers because inverters have better drive strength and slew characteristics for the same area. They also have lower input capacitance for the same drive. Using pairs maintains clock polarity. Some tools use clock buffers (CKBUFs) specifically designed for clock networks with symmetric rise/fall characteristics to minimize duty cycle distortion.
What is clock uncertainty? What components does it include?+
Clock uncertainty is a timing margin added to account for clock timing unpredictability. Components: (1) Jitter — cycle-to-cycle variation in clock period from PLL/oscillator (~20–50 ps). (2) Skew — residual skew after CTS balancing. (3) Crosstalk delta delay — aggressor nets causing clock net delay variation. In SDC: set_clock_uncertainty 0.1 -setup [get_clocks clk] adds 100ps of setup uncertainty. Post-CTS, uncertainty is usually split: larger for setup, smaller for hold.

11.4 Antenna Effect

During plasma etching in fabrication, long metal segments connected only to a gate (no diffusion nearby) accumulate charge, which can damage the thin gate oxide. The "antenna ratio" = metal area / gate area must stay below the foundry limit.

Antenna Fix: Jumper

Reroute the violating wire segment through a higher metal layer (which connects to diffusion first). Breaks the long metal-to-gate accumulation path.

Antenna Fix: Diode

Insert an antenna diode cell near the gate pin. The diode provides a discharge path for accumulated charge during fabrication without affecting circuit function.

11.5 Routing Congestion Handling

  • Layer promotion: Route congested nets on higher, less crowded layers
  • Cell spreading: Spread placement in congested regions (feedback to placement)
  • Net ordering: Route critical nets first to guarantee they get the best paths
  • Via stacking reduction: Minimize stacked vias which take up multiple layers' resources
Interview Questions — Routing
What is the difference between global routing and detailed routing?+
Global routing divides the chip into routing regions (GCells) and assigns each net to a path through these regions — determining which layers and general areas to use. It's a high-level capacity planning step. Detailed routing then places the actual wire geometry: specific tracks, exact coordinates, via placement, while obeying all DRC rules. Global routing is fast and guides detailed routing; detailed routing is slow and produces real GDS geometry.
What is an antenna violation? How is it fixed?+
During plasma etching, long metal segments connected to a transistor gate but not to diffusion act as antennas, accumulating charge. If the antenna ratio (metal area / gate oxide area) exceeds the foundry limit, the gate oxide can be damaged, causing reliability failures. Fixes: (1) Jump to a higher metal layer early in the route (connect to diffusion first at a lower layer, breaking the long accumulation). (2) Add an antenna protection diode near the gate pin to provide a safe discharge path.
Why do metal layers alternate routing directions?+
Adjacent metal layers are assigned perpendicular routing directions (e.g., M2=Vertical, M3=Horizontal) to prevent two parallel wires on adjacent layers from running side-by-side for long distances. Long parallel coupling creates: (1) High capacitive coupling → crosstalk noise and delay. (2) Increased risk of shorts from process variation. Alternating directions ensures most coupling is between perpendicular wires which have much shorter overlap lengths, minimizing both crosstalk and shorts.

12.2 Setup Timing Check

The setup check verifies that data arrives at the capture flip-flop's D pin before the clock edge, with sufficient margin (setup time of the FF).

Setup Timing Equation
Arrival Time (AT) = Launch FF clk-to-Q + Combinational Delay + Net Delay
Required Time (RT) = Clock Period + Capture FF Clock Arrival − Setup Time − Uncertainty
Setup Slack = RT − AT    (must be ≥ 0)
NEGATIVE SLACK = TIMING VIOLATIONIf slack is −50ps, the data arrives 50ps too late. The path must be sped up by 50ps through: buffer removal, gate sizing up, retiming, or reducing wire length.
Example calculation:
Clock period = 1000 ps
Launch FF CK→Q = 150 ps
Combinational delay = 500 ps
Net (wire) delay = 80 ps
─────────────────────────────
Arrival Time (AT) = 730 ps

Clock latency launch = 200 ps
Clock latency capture = 220 ps
FF Setup time = 50 ps
Clock uncertainty = 80 ps
─────────────────────────────
Required Time (RT) = 1000 + 220 − 50 − 80 = 1090 ps

Setup Slack = 1090 − 730 = +360 ps ✓ PASS
12.3 Hold Timing Check

The hold check verifies that data doesn't change at the capture FF's D pin too soon after the clock edge (before hold time elapses).

Hold Timing Equation
Arrival Time (AT) = Launch FF clk-to-Q (min) + Combinational Delay (min)
Required Time (RT) = Capture FF Clock Arrival (min) + Hold Time
Hold Slack = AT − RT    (must be ≥ 0)
HOLD VIOLATIONS ARE FATAL IN SILICONUnlike setup violations, you CANNOT fix hold by slowing the clock. Hold violations cause the wrong data to be captured, always. Fix: add delay buffers on the fast path. Hold fixing is done after CTS with real clock tree delays.

Most common. From launch FF (Q) through combinational logic to capture FF (D). Constrained by clock period minus all overhead.

Input-to-Reg Path

From chip input port through logic to first FF. Constrained by set_input_delay SDC constraint.

Reg-to-Output Path

From FF (Q) through logic to chip output port. Constrained by set_output_delay SDC constraint.

Input-to-Output (Comb)

Pure combinational path from input to output. Constrained by input + output delay budgets.

Interview Questions — STA
What is slack? What does negative slack mean?+
Slack is the timing margin — the difference between the required time and arrival time. Positive slack means the path meets timing with margin to spare. Negative slack means the path violates timing: the data arrives later than required (setup) or earlier than required (hold). The magnitude of negative slack tells you how much improvement is needed. WNS (Worst Negative Slack) = worst single path. TNS (Total Negative Slack) = sum of all negative slacks across all paths.
What is CRPR (Clock Reconvergence Pessimism Removal)?+
When the launch and capture clock paths share common clock tree segments, STA tools can be pessimistic by using different (best/worst) corners for the shared segment in setup and hold analysis. CRPR removes this common path pessimism by recognizing that the shared segment will have the same actual delay. CRPR credit is added back to the slack, making the analysis more realistic. Without CRPR, timing could appear to fail when it actually passes.
Explain OCV (On-Chip Variation) in STA.+
OCV accounts for the fact that transistors and wires at different locations on the same chip have slightly different characteristics due to process gradients, temperature gradients, and voltage drops. In STA, OCV is modeled by applying derating factors: the launch path cells are made slower (derate factor >1) and capture path cells faster for setup, or vice versa for hold. Advanced OCV includes AOCV (Advanced OCV — path-depth and distance dependent) and POCV (Parametric OCV — statistical variation modeling).

  • STA = exhaustive timing check of ALL paths without simulation vectors
  • Setup slack = RT − AT ≥ 0; setup uses slow/worst-case corner (SS)
  • Hold slack = AT − RT ≥ 0; hold uses fast/best-case corner (FF); cannot be fixed by slowing clock
  • Multiple PVT corners required: SS for setup, FF for hold, TT for nominal
  • WNS = worst path; TNS = total negative slack across design

13.1 Crosstalk

When two wires run parallel on the same metal layer, they form a capacitor between them. A switching signal (aggressor) couples noise onto its neighbour (victim), causing either a glitch or a delay change.

Crosstalk Effects
Crosstalk Glitch

Victim net is quiet, aggressor switches → voltage spike on victim. If spike crosses logic threshold, it causes a functional glitch (wrong value). Worst when victim is high-impedance (not driven strongly).

Crosstalk Delta Delay

Both victim and aggressor switch simultaneously. Same direction = faster (negative delta delay). Opposite direction = slower (positive delta delay). Can shift setup/hold timing by 50–200ps.

Interview Questions — Signal Integrity
What is the difference between crosstalk glitch and crosstalk delta delay?+
Crosstalk glitch occurs when the victim net is static (not switching) and the aggressor switches, inducing a voltage spike on the victim through mutual capacitance. If the spike crosses the logic threshold, it can cause a functional error. Crosstalk delta delay occurs when both victim and aggressor switch simultaneously — in the same direction it reduces victim delay (helpful but can cause hold violations), in opposite directions it increases victim delay (setup violation). Both are analyzed during signoff using tools like Synopsys PrimeTime SI.
What causes electromigration and how do you prevent it?+
EM is caused by electron wind force — fast-moving electrons in a wire transfer momentum to metal ions, gradually displacing them. This creates metal voids (resistance increase → open circuit) or hillocks (shorts to adjacent wires). Prevention: (1) Keep current density below foundry-specified Jmax. (2) Widen wires carrying high current (power nets, high-activity signal nets). (3) Use multiple parallel vias to distribute via current. (4) Thermal management — EM accelerates exponentially with temperature. (5) Run EM analysis using tools like Synopsys PrimeRail or Cadence Voltus.

Interview Questions — Physical Verification
What is the difference between DRC and LVS?+
DRC (Design Rule Check) verifies that the physical geometry of the layout satisfies the foundry's manufacturing rules — checking dimensions, spacings, areas, and densities. It does NOT check whether the circuit is correct. LVS (Layout vs Schematic) verifies that the physical connections in the layout match the intended circuit netlist — comparing transistors and connections. What is latch-up? How is it prevented in CMOS?+
Latch-up is a low-impedance path between VDD and GND caused by triggering of a parasitic PNPN thyristor structure inherent in CMOS. Once triggered, it draws massive current (potentially destroying the chip). Prevention: (1) Use guard rings (P+ diffusion around NMOS, N+ around PMOS) to collect injected minority carriers. (2) Maintain minimum spacing between NMOS and PMOS. (3) Connect substrate/well contacts frequently to keep bulk at proper potential. (4) ESD protection design. Modern standard cells already include guard ring structures.

15.1 What Are Parasitics?

Every wire in a chip has resistance (R) and capacitance (C) — these are parasitic elements not in the schematic but present in the physical layout. They cause delays, signal degradation, and power consumption that weren't present in pre-layout analysis.

Wire Resistance

R = ρ × L / (W × t)

  • ρ = resistivity of metal (Cu ~1.7 µΩ·cm)
  • L = wire length
  • W = wire width
  • t = metal thickness
  • Higher R → more IR drop, more RC delay
Wire Capacitance

C = ε × Area / d + fringe terms

  • Plate cap to adjacent layers
  • Fringe cap to nearby wires (coupling)
  • Higher C → slower switching, more power
  • Coupling C causes crosstalk
  • Dominant at lower metal layers

Coupling capacitance between adjacent wires has two effects in analysis:

Coupling cap treated as equivalent ground capacitance. Simple but pessimistic (doubles the effective coupling cap contribution). Used in non-SI STA analysis.

True Coupling (SI Analysis)

Aggressor/victim relationship modeled explicitly. More accurate. Allows crosstalk delta delay calculation. Required for SI-aware signoff (PrimeTime SI mode).

Interview Questions — Parasitic Extraction
Why does post-layout STA differ from pre-layout STA?+
Pre-layout STA uses statistical wire load models that estimate parasitics based on fanout and cell count. These can be off by 20–30% or more. Post-layout STA uses actual routed wire geometries extracted by a field solver (SPEF file), accounting for real wire lengths, widths, layer assignments, and coupling capacitances. The difference can cause paths that passed pre-route STA to fail after routing (added wire length/coupling from actual routing vs estimated) or vice versa. That's why we always re-run full STA after routing with SPEF.
What is SPEF reduction and why is it done?+
SPEF files for large chips can be hundreds of GB with millions of RC segments per net. SPEF reduction (or SPEF compression) merges or eliminates small RC elements that have negligible timing impact, reducing file size by 5–10× while maintaining timing accuracy within acceptable limits. The reduction is done by the extraction tool (e.g., Starrc) or a dedicated compression tool. Reduced SPEF significantly speeds up PrimeTime runtime without sacrificing signoff accuracy.

What is a GDS layer map file? Why is it needed?+

18.2 Key Fabrication Steps
Photolithography

The core patterning process. Light (or EUV) is shone through a photomask onto a photosensitive resist coating on the wafer. Where light hits, the resist changes chemical properties.

  • DUV (Deep UV, 193nm) — used for 28–7nm nodes
  • EUV (Extreme UV, 13.5nm) — used for 7nm and below
  • Multiple patterning required below 28nm (SADP, SAQP)
  • One mask = one layer exposure
Etching

Removes material from exposed areas. Two types:

  • Wet etching: Chemical solution etches isotropically. Simple but less precise.
  • Dry/Plasma etching: Ion bombardment — anisotropic (vertical only). Required for fine features.
Deposition

Adds material layers on the wafer:

  • CVD (Chemical Vapor Deposition): Deposits dielectrics (SiO₂, Si₃N₄)
  • PVD (Physical Vapor Deposition / Sputtering): Deposits metals (Al, Ti, TiN)
  • ELD (Electroplating): Copper damascene for interconnects
  • ALD (Atomic Layer Deposition): Ultra-thin, precise films for gate dielectric
CMP (Chemical Mechanical Polishing)

Planarizes (flattens) the wafer surface after each metal layer deposition. Without CMP, wafer becomes too uneven for subsequent lithography steps to be in focus.

This is why metal density rules exist — non-uniform density causes uneven CMP, leading to thickness variations and failures.

Interview Questions — Fabrication
What is EUV lithography and why is it needed below 7nm?+
EUV (Extreme Ultraviolet) lithography uses 13.5nm wavelength light — much shorter than DUV (193nm) — allowing much finer features to be patterned. Below 7nm, DUV requires 3–4 patterning passes per layer (SAQP) to achieve the required resolution, which is costly and increases cycle time dramatically. EUV can pattern in single or double exposure, reducing process complexity. ASML is the only supplier of EUV scanners (costing ~$170M each). TSMC, Samsung, and Intel use EUV for 7nm and below.
What is the difference between FinFET and planar MOSFET?+
Planar MOSFET (used down to ~28nm): gate sits on top of a flat silicon channel. As channels got shorter, the gate couldn't fully control the channel, leading to high leakage (short-channel effects). FinFET (16nm–7nm): the channel is a thin vertical "fin" of silicon, and the gate wraps around 3 sides of the fin — much better gate control, lower leakage, allows further scaling. Gate-All-Around (GAA, 3nm+): horizontal nanosheets with gate wrapping all 4 sides — even better control for the next generation. FinFETs enabled continuing Moore's Law below 28nm.

2. What is clock domain crossing (CDC)? How do you handle it?+
CDC occurs when a signal crosses from one clock domain to another with different frequencies or phases. Problems: metastability (signal sampled in undefined state), data coherence (multi-bit bus captured in different states). Solutions: (1) Single-bit: 2-FF synchronizer. (2) Multi-bit: FIFO with synchronized pointers (Gray code for pointer comparison). (3) Handshake protocol for low-bandwidth signals. (4) CDC analysis tools: Synopsys SpyGlass CDC, Cadence JasperGold CDC.
3. Difference between task and function in Verilog?+
Function: (1) Must return exactly one value. (2) Cannot contain timing controls (#, @, wait). (3) Cannot call tasks. (4) Used for combinational logic — synthesizable. Task: (1) Can have zero or more output/inout arguments, no return value. (2) Can contain timing controls. (3) Can call other tasks and functions. (4) Mainly used in testbenches, not synthesizable when they contain timing.
4. What is race condition in Verilog simulation? How to avoid?+
A race condition occurs when two always blocks schedule events at the same simulation time and the result depends on execution order (which the simulator doesn't guarantee). Classic example: using blocking assignments in clocked always blocks can cause read-before-write or write-before-read races. Solution: always use non-blocking (<=) in clocked always blocks — all RHS are evaluated before any LHS assignment, eliminating the race. This is why the golden rule exists.
Synthesis
5. What is the difference between set_max_delay and create_clock?+
create_clock defines a periodic clock on a port/pin — the tool analyzes all register-to-register paths clocked by this signal for setup (T−skew−uncertainty) and hold. set_max_delay defines a non-periodic point-to-point timing constraint on a specific path — overrides the default clock-based analysis for that path. set_max_delay is used for paths where the standard clock period doesn't apply, like CDC cut points or test paths, while still requiring a specific maximum delay.
6. What is dont_touch and dont_use in synthesis?+
dont_touch: Applied to a cell or net in the netlist — tells the synthesis/optimization tool to leave it exactly as-is. Used for: scan cells, cells in analog interfaces, manually optimized cells. dont_use: Applied to a library cell — tells the tool to never use this cell in any optimization. Used for: cells with known reliability issues, cells not available in the target technology flavor, or cells excluded for DFT reasons. dont_use is a library-level exclusion; dont_touch is a design-level preservation.
7. How does synthesis handle arithmetic operators like + and *?+
Addition (+) is typically synthesized as a ripple-carry adder or carry-lookahead adder by default. The tool may use architecture-level optimization to select the best adder topology for timing. Multiplication (*) maps to hardware multipliers: small multipliers use arrays of adders (Wallace tree or Dadda tree); large multipliers use dedicated DSP blocks if available. The synthesis tool can also perform arithmetic sharing — reusing adder/multiplier hardware across mutually exclusive operations to save area.
Physical Design
8. What is the physical design flow in order?+
Complete PD flow: (1) Floorplanning — die/core area, IO placement, macro placement, power planning. (2) Placement — standard cell global placement, legalization, timing-driven optimization. (3) CTS (Clock Tree Synthesis) — balanced clock tree, skew/latency targets. (4) Post-CTS optimization — fix setup/hold with real clock tree. (5) Routing — global + detailed routing, DRC clean. (6) Post-route optimization — ECO fixes, timing closure. (7) Physical verification — DRC, LVS, antenna. (8) Parasitic extraction — SPEF generation. (9) Signoff STA — PrimeTime with SPEF. (10) GDSII tapeout.
9. What is timing ECO? When is it needed?+
ECO (Engineering Change Order) is a targeted modification to fix specific violations without redoing the full PD flow. Timing ECO is done post-route when signoff STA reveals remaining setup/hold violations. Methods: (1) Cell sizing — swap cell to higher drive strength at same location. (2) Buffer insertion — add buffer to fix max transition/cap or drive strength. (3) Logic restructuring — change cell type (e.g., NAND to NOR) for different delay. (4) Wire spreading — reroute a net to a less congested area. ECO changes must be minimal to avoid disturbing already-clean areas of the design.
10. What is scan chain insertion? Why is it done?+
Scan chain insertion is a DFT (Design For Test) technique where all flip-flops are connected into a shift register chain. During test mode, the scan chain allows: (1) Shifting in known test patterns to all FFs. (2) Applying clock to propagate through combinational logic. (3) Shifting out results and comparing to expected values. Without scan, testing internal logic of a chip is nearly impossible after packaging. Scan coverage of 95%+ is typically required for volume production testing. Scan is inserted by synthesis tools (DC TetraMAX, Tessent).
STA & Timing
11. What is the timing equation for setup check? Derive it.+
Launch path AT = T_clk_launch + T_CKQ(launch_FF) + T_comb + T_net
Required Time RT = T_period + T_clk_capture − T_setup(capture_FF) − T_uncertainty
Setup slack = RT − AT ≥ 0
Expanded: T_period + T_clk_capture − T_clk_launch − T_setup − T_uncertainty ≥ T_CKQ + T_comb + T_net
The left side is the "budget" (clock period corrected for skew, setup, uncertainty). The right side is the "demand" (actual data path delay). If demand > budget, setup violation.
12. What is CPPR (Clock Path Pessimism Removal)? Same as CRPR?+
Yes — CPPR and CRPR are the same concept, just different naming conventions (Cadence uses CPPR, Synopsys uses CRPR). When launch and capture clock paths share common segments, the analysis can be pessimistic: for setup, the shared segment is made fast for capture but slow for launch. CPPR/CRPR removes this over-pessimism by recognizing the common segment has ONE real delay — not two different worst-case delays. The result is the shared-path delay is analyzed at one value, and the difference (pessimism credit) is added back to the slack.
13. What is the difference between TNS and WNS?+
WNS (Worst Negative Slack) = the single worst timing violation across the entire design — the most critical path. It tells you how far you are from meeting timing. TNS (Total Negative Slack) = sum of all negative slacks across all failing endpoints. TNS tells you how much total timing work remains. A design can have WNS of −50ps but TNS of −5000ps (100 paths each failing by 50ps). Fixing WNS alone won't close timing — you need to address the TNS. Signoff requires both WNS = 0 and TNS = 0.
CTS Questions
14. How does CTS affect hold timing?+
CTS introduces real clock tree delays. Before CTS, both launch and capture clocks were ideal (zero delay, zero skew). After CTS, if the capture FF's clock arrives earlier than the launch FF's clock (negative skew from the capture perspective), hold time becomes harder to meet because the capture clock edge comes earlier, giving less time for the data to remain stable after capture. This is why hold fixing (buffer insertion) is always done AFTER CTS, not before. Pre-CTS hold analysis is invalid.
15. What is the difference between local skew and global skew?+
Local skew: clock arrival time difference between launch and capture flip-flops of a specific timing path. This is what directly impacts that path's setup and hold slack. It's the most important metric for timing closure. Global skew: maximum clock arrival difference between ANY two flip-flops in the entire clock domain. This is a worst-case measure of how unbalanced the entire clock tree is. CTS targets low local skew for critical paths (often achievable even when global skew is larger) through useful skew techniques.
Routing & Signal Integrity
16. How do you fix hold violations post-route?+
Post-route hold violations are fixed by adding delay to the fast path. Methods: (1) Insert delay cells (dedicated delay buffer with matched rise/fall). (2) Insert and/or remove regular buffers to create delay. (3) Route the net on a higher-resistance metal layer for additional RC delay. (4) Use longer routing path intentionally. Important: hold fixes must not create new setup violations on the same or adjacent paths. The hold-fixing engine in the P&R tool (Innovus/ICC2) handles this automatically, but final checks must verify no new setup issues arose.
17. What is net shielding? When is it applied?+
Net shielding places GND or VDD wires on one or both sides of a critical signal wire to reduce coupling capacitance from aggressors. The shield wire is at fixed potential so any coupling from aggressors is absorbed by the shield rather than reaching the victim. Shielding is applied to: clock nets (protect from crosstalk delta delay), reset nets, high-speed signals with tight timing margins, and differential pairs requiring controlled impedance. Shielding uses extra routing resources (1–2 additional tracks), so it's used selectively on the most sensitive nets.
